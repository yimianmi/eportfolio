Designing Integrated Media Environments (EME 6208)

About This Portfolio

Welcome to my e-Portfolio for EME 6208: Designing Integrated Media Environments. Throughout this course, I explored how multimedia tools can support teaching, communication, and creative work. Before this class, I mainly worked in music composition, so learning web design, animation, and video editing opened a new area for expressing ideas.

This portfolio collects all the major projects I completed—image editing, interactive web activities, video production, digital animation, and a screencast. Each project taught me something different about how visual design, technology, and instruction work together. Building this final website also helped me understand how to organize content clearly and design a site that others can navigate easily.

Image Editing

Bitmap and Vector Imagery

In this project, I created a book cover and a personal logo using bitmap and vector techniques. I explored tools such as masking, layering, color blending, and contrast control. One goal was to understand how bitmap images work best for detailed textures, while vector graphics are ideal for clean shapes and scalable designs. Designing a book cover required choices about layout, balance, and focal points, while creating a logo taught me how simple shapes can communicate identity.

As someone who studies composition, the process felt similar to arranging musical textures. Just as different instruments create different colors, bitmap and vector formats each bring a distinct visual character. Learning how to combine them helped me think more clearly about visual storytelling. This project also helped me become comfortable with tools like cropping, adjustment layers, and typography systems, which I had rarely used before.

Through this assignment, I gained confidence in designing graphics that are clean, expressive, and ready for both print and digital use. It taught me the importance of clarity, consistency, and simplicity—principles that also matter when designing teaching materials or musical scores.

Web Design

Interactive Quiz & Extended Chords Quiz

For this project, I built two interactive learning tools: a 10-question music theory quiz and a drag-and-drop activity about extended chords (9th, 11th, and 13th). These two pages helped me understand how HTML provides structure, CSS controls style, and JavaScript adds interactivity. I designed a simple layout, adjusted colors, created buttons, and wrote code for instant feedback. The process required careful testing because even a small mistake could break the interaction.

As someone who teaches and writes music, I found it exciting to turn music-theory ideas into interactive learning tools. It made me think about how students learn best—through small steps, clear visuals, and trial-and-error exploration. Converting harmony concepts into drag-and-drop items helped me simplify the content and make it more accessible.

Publishing the projects through GitHub Pages was another important learning moment. I learned how to upload files, manage a repository, and generate a live website link. This skill will be very useful for building online teaching materials or hosting my own music-related projects in the future.

Video Production

“How Music Helps Us Relax”

For this assignment, I created a short educational video explaining how music affects relaxation. I used iMovie with its built-in transitions, the Ken Burns effect, background music, and recorded narration. My goal was to create a calm, gentle atmosphere, so I chose soft colors and slow pacing in both visuals and audio.

Working with audio layers felt familiar because of my music background, but syncing them with images required new skills. I learned how transitions shape emotional flow, how to balance narration with background music, and how to design sequences that feel smooth and meaningful. The process taught me how even small timing changes can improve clarity for learners.

Creating this video helped me understand how multimedia storytelling works and how educational content can be made more engaging through pacing, visuals, and sound. These skills will help me design future teaching videos about music and composition.

Animation

“How Velocity Works”

In this project, I created a short animation explaining the idea of velocity. I used Canva to design the shapes and layout, and then iMovie to add motion, narration, and timing. At first, I struggled to make the ball and arrows move naturally. Animation requires careful thinking about direction, timing, and visual rhythm—elements similar to phrasing in music.

I experimented with different frame pacing, object movement, and transitions to make the explanation easier to understand. Recording the narration also taught me how to keep explanations simple and clear for learners. This project made me more aware of how animation can help explain abstract concepts through visual cues and step-by-step motion.

In the end, I learned that animation does not need to be complicated to be effective. With thoughtful pacing and clean visuals, even a basic design can create a smooth and helpful learning experience.

Screencast

Sibelius Music Score Tutorial

For this screencast, I recorded a demonstration of how to create a basic music score in Sibelius on a Mac. I explained steps such as creating a new file, entering notes, editing rhythms, adding articulations, and using playback tools. I also used cursor highlighting and zooming to guide the viewer’s attention.

Making this screencast helped me understand the importance of clarity and pacing in instructional media. Although I use Sibelius often as a composer, teaching the software required a different perspective. I needed to slow down, make each step visible, and explain why certain tools matter. This process helped me appreciate how screencasts support visual and procedural learning.

Through this project, I learned how to plan a clear structure, prepare a script, and use editing tools to remove distractions. Recording my screen also gave me practical experience that I can use for teaching music notation or sharing composition workflows with students in the future.

E-Portfolio

E-Portfolio Reflection

Building this e-portfolio was one of the most meaningful parts of the course. I brought together all the projects—image editing, web design, video creation, animation, and screencasting—into one website that shows my growth throughout the semester. I learned how to structure a website, create consistent styling, design navigation, manage external files, and publish my work online.

Creating this portfolio also helped me think about how multimedia design connects with my work in music. As a composer, I often think about structure, balance, and flow. I realized that web design uses many of the same ideas: clear layout, visual hierarchy, color balance, and pacing. Organizing the projects into sections felt similar to organizing movements in a composition—each part has its own purpose but also contributes to the whole.

The portfolio helped me reflect on how technology can support teaching and creative communication. I now feel more confident using multimedia tools to design educational materials, present musical ideas, or share creative work online. This website represents not only what I learned from each project, but also how I grew in connecting design, technology, and my own artistic background.
